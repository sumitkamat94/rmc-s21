Broad area/overview

  • In this paper, first feedback control algorithm for applying a desired cartesian impedance on the end-point of a manipulator is presented. in this method, using inverse kinematic problem is not required any more.

  • Furthermore, The modulation of end-point Impedance without using feedback control is also considered, and it is shown that apparently "redundant" actuators and degrees of freedom may be used to modulate end-point impedance and may play an essential functional role in the control of dynamic interaction.

Notation

  • \(W(\theta)\): Mobility tensor

  • \(I\): Inertia

  • \(F_{int}\): interface force

  • \(:\{c\}\): Modulation by command set

  • \(T_{act}\):Actuator force or torque

  • \(K[.]\): Force /displacement relation

  • \(B[.]\): Force /velocity relation

  • \(J\): Jacobian

  • \(X\): End-point position

  • \(V\): End-point position

  • \(X_o\): Commanded position

  • \(V_o\): Commanded velocity

  • \(L(.)\): Linkage kinematic equation

  • \(G(\theta,\omega)\): accelerative coupling terms

  • \(Y(\theta)\): mobility tensor in end-point coordinate

  • \(S(\theta)\): position-dependent torques

  • \(M\): Inertia tensor in end-point coordinates

  • \(Y\): admittance

Specific Problem

  • The main idea is controlling the motion of manipulator and in addition providing a disturbance response to the system; thus, it will be deviated from that motion which has an impedance form. The dynamic of interaction maybe modulated or controlled by changing the impedance.

  • One alternative to feedback control which was discussed in this paper is the use of redundancies: "excess" actuators or "excess" skeletal degrees of freedom. From a purely kinematic standpoint the neuromuscular system is multiply redundant.

Basic Ideas and definitions

  • in impedance control, the lowest term which shows the relation between output force and input translation is a stiffness. The manipulator equations can be describes as follows:

(1) \(F_{int}=K[X_o-X]\)

Hence; the actuator torque can describes as follows:

(2) \(T_{act}=J^t(\theta)K[X_o-L(\theta)]\)

  • Another important term is showing the relation between the force and the velocity as the following:

(3) \(F_{int}=B[V_o-V]\)

thus, the torque can be calculated as the following:

(4) \(T_{act}=J^t(\theta)B[V_o-J(\theta)\omega]\)

  • In these equations, we did not consider the gravitational, frictional and inertial dynamics of the manipulator which is kind suitable for some applications, while in other applications it cannot be neglected.

  • If we consider the manipulator behavior based on time, velocity, displacement and the viscoelastic behavior the model can be described as the following:

(5) \(F_{int}=K[X_O-X]+B[V_o-V]-Mdv/dt\)

and the torque is described as the following:

(6) \(T_{act}=I(\theta)J^{-1}(\theta)M^{-1}K[X_oL(\theta)]+ S(\theta)+I(\theta)J^{-1}(\theta)M^{-1}B[V_o-J(\theta)\omega]+ V(\omega)+ I(\theta)J^{-1}(\theta)M^{-1}F_{int}-J^t(\theta)F_int-I(\theta)J^{-1}(\theta)G(\theta,\omega)-C(\theta,\omega)\) The input variables are the desired position and the terms of the desired dynamic behavior characterized by \(M\), \(B[•]\) and \(K[•]\).

  • The controller of equation (6) was designed by a technique which is similar to pole-placement methods.

  • an alternative to feedback is the use of redundancies: "excess" actuators or "excess" skeletal degrees of freedom. These skeletal redundancies can serve to provide a measure of control over the inertial component of the endpoint dynamics. In this form, the mobility tensor can be described as below:

(7) \(W(\theta)=J(\theta)Y(\theta)J^t(\theta)\)

  • By using intrinsic properties of the actuators , it is possible to modulate the position and velocity of the end-point impedance without using any feedback. It would be really important for industrial robots because computation time is an important factor in designing the controllers.

Comments

  • An improved understanding of how humans control the impedance of the lower limbs is necessary to optimize the mechanical design and control

of devices.

  • Impedance control add nonlinearity to the equations which make them more complex.
